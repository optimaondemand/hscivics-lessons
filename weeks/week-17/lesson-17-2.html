Lesson 17.2
Containment, the New World Order, and Resources as a Driver of Foreign Policy
📖 What You Are Reading Today

Topic: Two presidential addresses, forty-three years apart, bookend the Cold War. Truman's 1947 speech commits the United States to containment at the Cold War's opening; George H.W. Bush's 1990 address to Congress imagines what comes after, as the Cold War closes. Read both as arguments blending realist calculation with idealist justification, and then examine a required case study in how a very unglamorous, very concrete factor, control of strategic resources, has shaped decisions like these as much as any grand theory has.

🎯 By the End of This Lesson You Will Be Able To…
1
Explain containment as the doctrine announced in the Truman Doctrine, and identify its realist and idealist components.
2
Explain Bush's vision of a post-Cold War "new world order" built on collective security, and connect it to the Gulf War context in which it was announced.
3
Explain how control of and access to strategic resources (especially Middle Eastern oil) has shaped concrete U.S. foreign policy decisions across the Cold War and post-Cold War periods, as a case study in geography's influence on foreign policy.

Historians now use the word containment as shorthand for the policy Truman announced, almost in passing, in the speech you read on Tuesday: the United States would use its economic, diplomatic, and eventually military power to stop the further spread of Soviet-aligned communist power, without necessarily trying to roll back or destroy it where it already existed. The term itself comes from a slightly earlier, even more influential document; the diplomat George Kennan's 1946 "Long Telegram" and his follow-up 1947 article arguing that Soviet expansion had to be met with "long-term, patient but firm and vigilant containment." Truman's speech translated Kennan's strategic analysis into a public, congressional commitment, and in doing so set American foreign policy on a track it would follow, with variations, for the next four decades: in Korea, in Berlin, in Vietnam, in a hundred smaller confrontations across the globe.

Notice how the Truman Doctrine you read on Tuesday works as a blend of two of this week's five categories. Its realist core is plain enough once you look for it: a Soviet-aligned communist victory in Greece would put pressure on Turkey, threaten to "spread confusion and disorder" through the Middle East, and damage American strategic interests in a region increasingly important to American planners. But Truman does not sell this to Congress in the flat language of strategic calculation alone. He wraps it in the idealist language of a global contest between "alternative ways of life"; a language that turns a specific, limited aid request into a moral commitment to "free peoples" everywhere. That fusion of hard calculation and moral framing is exactly why the speech worked as persuasion, and exactly why it became a doctrine rather than a one-time appropriation.

Bush and the "New World Order"

By 1990, the world Truman had been responding to was ending. The Soviet Union's grip on Eastern Europe was collapsing, the Berlin Wall had fallen the previous year, and the Cold War's basic organizing conflict (two superpowers, two blocs, one long standoff) no longer described the world accurately. Into this moment, President George H.W. Bush faced an immediate crisis: in August 1990, Iraq under Saddam Hussein invaded and annexed its small, oil-rich neighbor Kuwait. Bush assembled an extraordinary international coalition, including the Soviet Union itself and several Arab states, backed by a United Nations Security Council resolution authorizing force, to reverse the invasion; a campaign that became the Gulf War of early 1991.

In an address to Congress in September 1990, as that coalition was being built, Bush described the moment in terms that went well beyond Kuwait. He spoke of "a rare opportunity" to move toward what he called a new world order; a phrase he used to describe an international system in which nations would act together, through cooperative and collective security arrangements rather than unilateral aggression, to deter and reverse acts like Iraq's invasion. Bush framed the Gulf crisis as an early test of whether the post-Cold War world could actually operate on those terms: whether naked aggression against a small state would be tolerated by the international community, or answered collectively.

Read Bush's vision alongside Truman's, and the family resemblance is unmistakable. Both blend realist and idealist reasoning. The realist core of Bush's position was straightforward: Iraq's conquest of Kuwait put Saddam Hussein in a position to dominate a large share of the world's oil reserves and to threaten Saudi Arabia, a far larger prize and a far more consequential American strategic partner. But Bush, like Truman, did not rest his public case on oil and strategic access alone. He framed the response in the language of collective security and the rule of law among nations; aggression must not be allowed to pay, sovereignty must be respected, coalitions of free nations must act together against outlaw states. Realist interest and idealist principle, once again, pointing in the same direction and reinforcing each other in the public argument for action.

Case Study: Strategic Resources and the Shape of American Foreign Policy

Foreign policy is not made only in the language of doctrines and moral principle. It is also made on a map, in relation to where the world's most valuable and most necessary resources happen to sit. This is a case study in geography's influence on foreign policy; the kind of analysis geographers perform when they study how resource distribution shapes human decisions, including decisions about war and diplomacy.

The Gulf War is the clearest illustration available to you this week. The Persian Gulf region holds a disproportionate share of the world's proven oil reserves, and by the late twentieth century, the industrialized world (the United States very much included) depended on reliable access to that oil for its economy to function at all. When Iraq seized Kuwait in 1990, it was not simply annexing territory; it was placing a hostile, unpredictable regime in a position to control an enormous share of global oil supply and to threaten Saudi Arabia's much larger reserves next door. That fact, more than any abstract commitment to international law, is why the crisis drew an American-led coalition so quickly and so decisively. Notice, too, how directly this connects back to Truman's own words in 1947: he warned that if Greece fell, "confusion and disorder might well spread throughout the entire Middle East"; the region's strategic and resource importance to American planners was already assumed, decades before Kuwait.

Oil is the central example this week, but it is not the only recurring case of resources shaping policy. Access to fresh water has driven tension and diplomacy in the Middle East, the Nile basin, and Central Asia for decades. Control of strategic minerals (cobalt, lithium, rare earth elements used in modern electronics, batteries, and military technology) has become an increasingly visible factor in how the United States and other powers weigh their relationships with resource-producing regions in Africa, Latin America, and Asia. In each case, the same underlying pattern holds: where a critical resource is concentrated geographically, the nations that need it develop foreign policies shaped, in part, by the location of that resource on the map; whether those policies are dressed in the language of realism, idealism, or both at once.

Hold all three pieces of this lesson together: containment as the Cold War's opening doctrine, the "new world order" as its closing vision, and strategic resources as the quiet, recurring, unglamorous factor that shaped how both were actually applied on the ground. Grand doctrines explain why American leaders said they were acting. Geography, more often than we like to admit, explains where and when they actually did.
