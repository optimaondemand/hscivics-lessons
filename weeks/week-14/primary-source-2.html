📖 Context Before You Read

Chief Justice Earl Warren (the same Chief Justice who wrote Miranda two years earlier) also wrote the Court's opinion in Terry v. Ohio. The case asked whether a police officer who stops and pats down a person based on suspicion falling short of probable cause has violated the Fourth Amendment. Read for the new standard the Court creates, and for how carefully the Court limits it.

"[A] police officer may in appropriate circumstances and in an appropriate manner approach a person for purposes of investigating possibly criminal behavior even though there is no probable cause to make an arrest. [When] an officer is justified in believing that the individual whose suspicious behavior he is investigating at close range is armed and presently dangerous to the officer or to others, it would appear to be clearly unreasonable to deny the officer the power to take necessary measures to determine whether the person is in fact carrying a weapon and to neutralize the threat of physical harm."

"In justifying the particular intrusion the police officer must be able to point to specific and articulable facts which, taken together with rational inferences from those facts, reasonably warrant that intrusion... in determining whether the officer acted reasonably... due weight must be given, not to his inchoate and unparticularized suspicion or 'hunch,' but to the specific reasonable inferences which he is entitled to draw from the facts in light of his experience."

"The sole justification of the search in the present situation is the protection of the police officer and others nearby, and it must therefore be confined in scope to an intrusion reasonably designed to discover guns, knives, clubs, or other hidden instruments for the assault of the police officer... Officer McFadden patted down the outer clothing of petitioner and his two companions. He did not place his hands in their pockets or under the outer surface of their garments until he had felt weapons, and then he merely reached for and removed the guns."

Terry v. Ohio, 392 U.S. 1, 21, 27, 29–30 (1968) (Warren, C.J.). Official U.S. Supreme Court opinion; public domain.
💡 Why a Lower Standard for a Smaller Intrusion?

Notice that Terry does not lower the standard for every kind of police action; only for the specific, limited action of a brief stop and a protective pat-down. A full arrest or a full search of a person's belongings still requires probable cause. Keep this distinction in mind as you answer the questions in Primary Source Report 14.1: the size of the intrusion and the strength of the justification required to authorize it move together.
