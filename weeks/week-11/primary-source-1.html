📖 Context Before You Read

This is the ratified text of Article III in full; short enough to read in one sitting, unlike Articles I and II. Read it the way you have read the earlier articles: slowly, clause by clause, asking what design question each provision answers. This is a lighter reading-guide format this quiz week rather than a formal Critical Questions assessment; annotations are provided, and the questions below are ungraded discussion prompts.

Section 1. "The judicial Power of the United States, shall be vested in one supreme Court, and in such inferior Courts as the Congress may from time to time ordain and establish. The Judges, both of the supreme and inferior Courts, shall hold their offices during good Behaviour, and shall, at stated Times, receive for their Services a Compensation which shall not be diminished during their Continuance in Office."

Annotation: One Supreme Court by constitutional right; every lower court by congressional choice, first exercised in the Judiciary Act of 1789. Then the two independence protections discussed in the lesson: life tenure during good behaviour, and salary that cannot be cut.

Section 2 (selected). "The judicial Power shall extend to all Cases, in Law and Equity, arising under this Constitution, the Laws of the United States, and Treaties made, or which shall be made, under their Authority… to Controversies between two or more States… between Citizens of different States… In all Cases affecting Ambassadors, other public Ministers and Consuls, and those in which a State shall be Party, the supreme Court shall have original Jurisdiction. In all the other Cases before mentioned, the supreme Court shall have appellate Jurisdiction… The trial of all Crimes, except in Cases of Impeachment, shall be by Jury…"

Annotation: The categories of case within federal judicial power, the original-versus-appellate jurisdiction split discussed in the lesson, and the constitutional jury-trial guarantee for criminal cases.

Section 3. "Treason against the United States, shall consist only in levying War against them, or in adhering to their Enemies, giving them Aid and Comfort. No Person shall be convicted of Treason unless on the Testimony of two Witnesses to the same overt Act, or on Confession in open Court."

Annotation: A narrow, precisely bounded definition of treason, paired with a demanding evidence standard: a deliberate guard against the loose treason accusations the framers associated with the English Crown.

💡 Check Your Understanding

Answer the following for yourself or in class discussion. These are ungraded; there is no single correct answer to most of them, and no rubric attached. Their purpose is to make sure Article III's text has actually landed before you move to Wednesday's lesson on judicial review.

1
Article III spends far fewer words building the judiciary than Article I spends on Congress. Does that brevity suggest the framers thought the judicial branch mattered less, or does something else explain the difference in length? Defend your reading.
2
Section 1's two protections (tenure and salary) both work by shielding judges from the other two branches. Which branch (Congress or the President) does each protection most directly guard against, and why might the framers have worried about that branch specifically?
3
Section 3's treason clause is unusually specific compared to most of Article III's language. Why might the framers have wanted to pin down this one crime so precisely in the Constitution itself, rather than leaving its definition to ordinary statute the way they left most federal crimes?
