📖 Context Before You Read

This treatise on the Athenian constitution was recovered on an Egyptian papyrus in 1890, after being lost for over two thousand years; it is attributed to Aristotle or a student writing under his direction around 330 BC, roughly two and a half centuries after Solon's archonship. The excerpt below covers the charge that Solon profited personally from the Seisachtheia, and his own account (through the historian's paraphrase) of what he did and did not do with the power Athens gave him. Read for the case Aristotle builds in Solon's defense, and for what Solon's own poetry (quoted within the text) tells you about how he wanted to be remembered.

"Now, Solon, when he had got to be at the head of affairs, made the people free both for the present and the future, by forbidding loans on the security of the person, and he made laws, and a cancelling of all debts both private and public; this they call Seisachtheia (the disburdening ordinance), as having shaken off their burden. It is in regard to these measures that men try to attack his character. For it happened that when Solon was about to make the Seisachtheia, he announced it first to some of the upper class, and then, as the popular side say, his friends stole a march upon him... while the possessors of property bring the injurious charge that he made a profit himself.

Not but what the account of the popular side is the more trustworthy; for it is not reasonable that in all other respects he should have shown himself so moderate and impartial — while it rested entirely with himself whether... he would, by introducing his laws in an underhand way, make himself master of the state... it is not reasonable, I say, to suppose that in such petty and unworthy matters he would defile himself."

"...the following are the three provisions of the constitution of Solon which appear to be the most favourable to the people: first and foremost, the prohibition of loans on the security of the person; then the right accorded to anyone who wished to seek in the courts a remedy for his wrongs; and third (by which, most of all, they say the masses have acquired power), the right of appeal to the court of justice; for when the people is master of the vote, it becomes master of the government."

Aristotle (attrib.), Constitution of Athens, trans. Thomas J. Dymes (London: Seeley and Co., 1891), ch. VI & IX. Public domain.
