Solon and the Birth of Athenian Democracy
📖 What You Are Reading Today

Constitution of Athens

Attributed to Aristotle, ca. 330 BC

Reading: An account of how Athens moved from a narrow rule of birth toward government answerable to its citizens: the beginning of a six-week study of what a citizen is, and why the American founders kept returning to Athens and Rome for their answer.

🎯 By the End of This Lesson You Will Be Able To…
1
Explain why the Athenian constitution before Solon protected the few at the expense of the many, and identify what specifically Solon changed.
2
Define oligarchy, timocracy, and democracy and place Athens's early constitutions on that spectrum.
3
State the six foundational political questions this course returns to all semester, and connect Solon's reforms to at least two of them.

Begin with a debt. Not a metaphor: an actual debt, owed by an actual farmer, to an actual man richer than he was. In Athens, in the years before 594 BC, a poor farmer who could not pay what he owed could be seized, along with his wife and children, and made to work the creditor's land as bond-servants. If the debt still wasn't paid, he could be sold outright. The Greeks had a word for this arrangement, hektemoroi, "sixth-parters," named for the sixth of the harvest a tenant owed his landlord as rent. Fail to pay that rent, and the law offered the landlord your body as security.

This is not the Athens most people picture when they hear the word democracy (itself a Greek compound, demos, the people, plus kratos, power or rule). The Athens of 600 BC was what Aristotle's school would later call an oligarchy: rule by the few, in this case a hereditary nobility, the Eupatridae ("the well-born"), who held the offices of state (the archons who governed the city, the Areopagus that heard cases and guarded the laws) by birth, not by wealth or merit. The dividing line in early Athens was not rich versus poor. It was Aristocrat versus commoner, and "commoner" covered a wide range of fortunes. At the bottom sat the hektemoroi described above, genuinely poor and in real danger of losing their freedom. But well above them stood a substantial middle rank of commoners, the sort of men who would soon be classed as zeugitai: yeoman farmers who owned and worked a modest plot with their own hands, neither renting land from a landlord nor employing tenants of their own. Some commoners, especially those who had done well through trade, were wealthier still, in a few cases as wealthy as any Eupatrid family. What excluded all of them from office was not the size of their harvest. It was the accident of not having been born into the right handful of families.

Every political community answers the question who rules, and on what basis? differently, and Athens's answer, for its first two centuries, was simply: birth decides, because it always has. A commoner's wealth, however great, bought him no share in governing. That is not a constitutional theory. It is inertia given the appearance of order. The interesting history begins when Athens stopped accepting that answer.

The Archon Who Refused a Crown

By the early 590s BC, Athens stood at the edge of civil war. The poorest commoners, crushed by debt-bondage, wanted the land redivided. The Aristocracy, holding both the land and the offices of state, wanted nothing to change. Both sides, remarkably, agreed to hand the problem to one man: Solon, himself of aristocratic blood, though not from among the wealthiest of the great Eupatrid houses. "Moderate means," though, undersells him: Solon was a merchant who made his own fortune trading across the Aegean both before and after his archonship, journeys that were not the ventures of a poor man, and he was already known across the city for his poetry and his sharp tongue. He was elected archon in 594 BC with something close to emergency powers: the authority to rewrite the constitution.

Solon did not give the poorest commoners what they wanted most (a redistribution of the land) nor the Aristocracy what it wanted most (no change at all). Instead he cancelled the existing debts outright (a measure the Athenians called the Seisachtheia, the "shaking-off of burdens") and permanently outlawed the practice of using a person's own body as collateral for a loan. No Athenian could again be enslaved for debt. He went further still: where Athenians had already been sold into slavery abroad before his reforms took effect, the state tracked down a number of them, purchased their freedom at public expense, and brought them home. He then reorganized eligibility for office around a new principle: not birth, but a graduated scale of wealth. Athenians were divided into four property classes, from the Pentakosiomedimnoi (those whose land produced at least 500 measures of grain, oil, or wine a year) down to the Thetes, the poorest, who owned little or no land at all. Between the two sat the zeugitai, the "yoke-men," Athens's middle class of yeoman farmers. The top three classes, no longer only the Eupatridae, could now hold office and sit in a new Council of Four Hundred, which meant wealthy and middling commoners alike sat in a governing body for the first time in Athenian history. The Thetes still could not hold office. But Solon gave every citizen, including the Thetes, a seat on the new courts of the Heliaia, so that any citizen, however poor, could appeal a magistrate's ruling to a jury of his fellow citizens.

Notice what Solon did not do. He did not make Athens a democracy in the sense we would use the word. Office-holding still depended on property, and the Eupatridae still dominated the archonship and the Areopagus in practice, since only the wealthy could afford the unpaid burden of office. Every male citizen, whatever his class, had always been entitled to attend the Assembly; that ancient institution was never in dispute, before Solon or after him. What Solon actually gave the Thetes, for the first time, was a vote on the jury that judged their neighbors and their magistrates. Aristotle, writing a century and a half later, singled out that right of appeal to a jury of one's peers as the single most consequential of Solon's reforms, more consequential, he thought, even than cancelling the debts, because it planted the seed from which everything recognizably democratic in Athens later grew.

When his work was done, Solon did something almost no one in his position ever does: he left. Athenian law let him rule as he liked for as long as he liked, and both the Aristocracy and the poorest commoners by turns tried to talk him into becoming their champion against the other. He refused both offers, left the city for ten years so that no one could pressure him to revise his own laws, and let Athens live under what he had written, unrevised by its author. He later wrote that he had "stood with my shield thrown round both sides alike, and let neither triumph unjustly over the other," a self-description modern historians have generally found more accurate than self-serving.

A Question Older Than Athens, and Newer Than Yesterday

Every constitutional order has to answer a small set of recurring questions: Who rules, and what makes that rule legitimate? How is authority distributed among citizens and the communities they belong to? What makes people willingly obey the law and bring their disputes to the courts rather than settling them by force? This course will return to these questions in one form or another almost every week between now and December, through Rome, through Locke and Montesquieu, through Philadelphia in 1787, through the Federalist Papers, and eventually through the Bill of Rights and a string of Supreme Court cases that are still being argued about today.

Solon matters to that story for a specific reason. James Madison and the other framers of the U.S. Constitution were trained in classical literature the way modern students are trained in mathematics: it was assumed, foundational, everywhere. When Madison sat down in 1787 to think about how to distribute power in a government answerable to its citizens, he was not inventing the problem. He had Athens, and Rome after it, in view as case studies of what happens when a political community tries, and sometimes fails, to answer that same question well. Solon's Athens is the first surviving case in the Western tradition of a community that looked at its own crumbling legitimacy and chose reform over either revolution or repression. That is why we start here.
