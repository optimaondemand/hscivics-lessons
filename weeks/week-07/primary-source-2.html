📖 Context Before You Read

Thucydides wrote his history of the Peloponnesian War as a participant and eyewitness, exiled from Athenian command partway through the conflict, determined to record events with as much precision and as little embellishment as he could manage; his stated aim was a history useful to future readers trying to understand human political behavior, not merely a memorable story. Books VI and VII recount the Athenian debate over invading Sicily and the two years of campaigning that followed, ending in the near-total destruction of the expedition. Read for Nicias's warning, Alcibiades's answer, and Thucydides's own verdict on the outcome, delivered in his own voice as historian rather than through either speaker.

Nicias, urging restraint: "I know that... those who wish to be thought foremost in the state, do not allow such a one to maintain his private splendour at his country's risk... this is a matter of importance, and not for a young man to decide or hastily to take in hand." [Warning against the ambition of the expedition's younger advocates.]

Alcibiades, in reply: "my conviction is that a city not inactive by nature could not choose a quicker way to ruin itself than by suddenly adopting such a policy [of caution], and that the safest rule of life is to take one's character and institutions for better and for worse, and to live up to them as closely as one can." After hearing him, "the Athenians became more eager for the expedition than before."

Of the expedition's final destruction two years later, Thucydides writes in his own voice: "This was the greatest Hellenic achievement of any in this war, or, in my opinion, in Hellenic history; at once most glorious to the victors, and most calamitous to the conquered. They were beaten at all points and altogether; all that they suffered was great; they were destroyed, as the saying is, with a total destruction, their fleet, their army, everything was destroyed, and few out of many returned home."

Thucydides, History of the Peloponnesian War, Book 6, chs. 12–24, and Book 7, ch. 87, trans. Richard Crawley (1874). Public domain.
💡 Why This Counts as a Primary Source

Thucydides was alive during the war he describes and had access to participants on both sides; ancient and modern historians alike treat his history as one of the closest things we have to a primary account of these events, even though it is a historian's narrative rather than a transcript or a law. Compare that status to Madison's own essay; one is a participant's history written soon after the fact, the other a persuasive essay written by an actor arguing for a specific outcome. Both are primary sources; neither is a neutral summary.
