Lesson 7.2
Federalism as Double Security, and a Democracy That Voted Itself Into Disaster
📖 What You Are Reading Today

Topic: Madison's solution to faction has a second, vertical dimension beyond the extended republic; the division of power between national and state governments. Then a case study from fifth-century Athens: a real assembly, voting directly and passionately, that committed an entire city to catastrophe in a single afternoon.

🎯 By the End of This Lesson You Will Be Able To…
1
Explain federalism as a "double security" that divides power between national and state governments, and connect it to this week's discussion topic of separation of powers.
2
Narrate the Athenian Assembly's decision to invade Sicily in 415 BC, including the roles of Alcibiades and Nicias.
3
Explain why Thucydides's account of the Sicilian Expedition illustrates, in concrete historical form, exactly the danger Madison warns against in Federalist No. 10.

This week's discussion topic asks you to think about Madison on separation of powers; the horizontal division of the national government into legislative, executive, and judicial branches, each checking the others. Federalist No. 10 gives you the logic behind a second, equally important division: not horizontal, but vertical. Federalism is the arrangement by which political power is divided between a national government and the state governments beneath it, each sovereign in its own sphere and each a check on the ambitions of the other.

Madison develops this argument at length elsewhere in the Federalist Papers (most explicitly in No. 51, which you will encounter later this semester), but the logic is already implicit in No. 10's case for an extended republic. If a large, diverse republic controls the effects of faction better than a small one, that logic applies at two levels simultaneously. The national government, spanning the whole diverse union, is structurally resistant to any single faction seizing permanent control, for exactly the reasons No. 10 lays out. But Madison does not therefore conclude that all power should simply be centralized in that national government. Instead the Constitution splits authority between the nation and the states, so that a faction that manages to capture a state government still runs into the limits of that state's power, and a faction that somehow captured the national government would still find the states retaining independent authority of their own. Madison later called this arrangement a "double security" for the rights of the people: "the different governments will control each other, at the same time that each will be controlled by itself." Federalism, in other words, is separation of powers applied to the nation/state axis rather than the executive/legislative/judicial axis; another structural answer to the same underlying problem of unchecked power, whether that power belongs to a king, a faction, or an unrestrained majority.

A Vote That Could Not Be Unmade

Everything Madison feared about a direct democracy acting on the passion of the moment had already happened, in vivid and well-documented form, nearly twenty-two centuries before he wrote. The Athenian historian Thucydides, who lived through the Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC) and wrote its history as both participant and analyst, left behind an account of one of the most consequential single votes in Greek history: the Athenian Assembly's decision, in 415 BC, to launch a massive invasion of Sicily.

By 415 BC Athens had been fighting Sparta and its allies, on and off, for sixteen years, under an uneasy truce. A request for help arrived from Segesta, a small city in Sicily embroiled in a local war with Syracuse, the island's dominant power. On its face, this was a modest opportunity: send a small fleet, support an ally, perhaps check Syracuse's growing strength before it could ever aid Sparta. But the request landed in front of an Assembly primed for something much larger, and two very different men rose to shape what the city would decide to do.

Nicias, an experienced and cautious general, opposed the expedition altogether. He warned the Assembly that they were underestimating Sicily's size, wealth, and military strength, that Athens could not adequately supply and reinforce a war fought so far from home while Sparta still threatened them nearby, and that the whole scheme was being driven less by sound strategy than by appetite; particularly the appetite of the young men eager for glory and plunder. Thucydides has Nicias tell the Assembly plainly that they were about to go to war with people who had done them no wrong, on the basis of wishful thinking about easy victory.

Alcibiades, a brilliant, ambitious, and famously charismatic young Athenian politician, argued the opposite case, and argued it far more persuasively. He appealed to Athenian pride and appetite for expansion, painted Sicily as disorganized and ripe for the taking, and framed caution itself as a kind of national weakness unworthy of Athens's power. Thucydides is candid that Alcibiades's motives were not purely patriotic; he was hungry for command, for wealth, and for the personal glory a Sicilian conquest would bring him. Rather than talking the Assembly out of the expedition, Nicias's warnings backfired: when he tried to make the citizens reconsider by describing just how large a force the mission would actually require, the Assembly responded by voting him an even larger one, seemingly more excited by the scale of the enterprise than sobered by it.

The Assembly voted to send the expedition; ultimately more than a hundred ships and tens of thousands of men, one of the largest forces Athens ever committed to a single campaign. Thucydides is direct about the effect of the debate itself: after hearing Alcibiades and the Egestaean envoys, "the Athenians became more eager for the expedition than before," a citizen body whose passions, if anything, grew with each round of argument rather than cooling under scrutiny.

What the Vote Cost

The expedition was, in the end, a total catastrophe. The siege of Syracuse dragged on for two years, hampered by divided and cautious command after Alcibiades himself was recalled to Athens on a corruption charge and defected to Sparta rather than face trial. Athenian reinforcements arrived too late and in circumstances too degraded to save the campaign. By 413 BC, the Athenian fleet had been destroyed in the harbor of Syracuse, and the retreating army was surrounded, and its survivors killed or sold into slavery. Thucydides's account of the final defeat is unflinching about the scale of the loss: as he puts it, of the many thousands who went, "few out of many returned home." The disaster gutted Athens's navy, drained its treasury, and emboldened Sparta and its allies at the worst possible moment; historians generally treat the Sicilian Expedition as the single most consequential turning point of the entire war, tipping a long, grinding conflict decisively in Sparta's favor.

Notice what actually happened, structurally, in the Assembly that day. There was no upper house to slow the vote down, no separate executive with an independent check, no requirement of supermajority consent, no division between a national and a more local authority that might have limited the scale of the commitment. A single body of citizens, meeting once, swept up in the rhetoric of an ambitious and self-interested speaker, voted the entire community into an irreversible commitment that its own most experienced general had warned against in detail. This is not a metaphorical illustration of Madison's argument in Federalist No. 10. It is close to a textbook case of it: a direct democracy, small enough and unified enough that a passionate temporary majority could seize the whole apparatus of the state in a single session, with nothing built into the structure of Athenian government to slow the decision down, divide the decision-making power, or force reconsideration once the passion of the moment had passed. Madison never cites this specific episode (he did not need to; the pattern was familiar to any educated reader of Thucydides in his era), but the logic is exactly his: extend the sphere, divide the power, multiply the interests that must agree before the whole community can be committed to any single course, precisely because you cannot count on the wisdom or restraint of any single assembly, however confident, to check its own worst impulses in the moment that matters most.
