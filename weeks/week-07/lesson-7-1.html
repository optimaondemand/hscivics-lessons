Lesson 7.1
Publius Writes for New York: Madison and the Problem of Faction
📖 What You Are Reading Today

The Federalist, Nos. 9–10

Alexander Hamilton and James Madison, 1787–88

Reading: Who "Publius" was, why the Federalist Papers exist, and Madison's answer to a question every republic has to answer: what do you do about the fact that free people will always divide into competing groups pursuing their own interest at the expense of everyone else's?

🎯 By the End of This Lesson You Will Be Able To…
1
Explain what the Federalist Papers are, who wrote them under the name "Publius," and why they were written, and identify why that purpose means they should be read as advocacy, not neutral commentary.
2
State Madison's definition of faction in Federalist No. 10 and explain his argument that faction cannot be eliminated without destroying liberty itself.
3
Explain Madison's solution (the extended republic), and why he judged it superior to a small direct democracy, and connect his reasoning to the Rousseau lesson from Week 4.

In the autumn of 1787, the Constitutional Convention in Philadelphia had finished its work and sent the proposed Constitution out to the states for ratification. New York's ratification was far from certain. The state's governor, George Clinton, opposed the new charter, and New York's newspapers were filling with essays attacking it as a document that would swallow the states, endanger liberty, and hand power to a distant, unaccountable government. Between October 1787 and August 1788, eighty-five essays appeared in New York papers answering those attacks, arguing point by point for the Constitution's ratification. All eighty-five were signed with a single pen name: Publius, after Publius Valerius Publicola, a founder of the Roman Republic remembered for voluntarily giving up power once it had served its purpose. Behind that one name stood three men: Alexander Hamilton, who wrote the largest share; James Madison, who wrote the essays this week's lesson centers on; and John Jay, who wrote a handful before illness forced him to stop.

It matters, before you read a word of Publius, to be honest about what these essays are. They are not a neutral explanation of the Constitution written by disinterested scholars after the fact. Hamilton and Madison had both been delegates at the Philadelphia convention; they had helped write the document they were now defending, and they had a direct stake in seeing it ratified. The Federalist Papers are a work of persuasion, aimed at a specific and skeptical audience (the delegates who would soon vote at New York's ratifying convention) written by people who already believed the Constitution should pass. That does not make them dishonest, and it does not make their arguments weak; Madison's reasoning in the essay you'll read today has outlasted nearly every specific political fight of 1787–88 and is still taught as serious political theory. But you should read Publius the way you'd read a skilled lawyer's brief: rigorous, often brilliant, and never pretending to be anything other than one side's case.

A Danger Every Republic Has to Face

In Federalist No. 9, Hamilton opened a sequence of essays on a specific worry raised by the Constitution's opponents: that republics are inherently unstable, doomed to collapse into the "tempestuous waves of sedition and party rage" that had wrecked ancient and Italian republics before. Madison picked up that thread directly in Federalist No. 10, arguably the single most studied essay in the entire collection. His opening move is definitional, and precise. "By a faction," he writes, "I understand a number of citizens, whether amounting to a majority or minority of the whole, who are united and actuated by some common impulse of passion, or of interest, adverse to the rights of other citizens, or to the permanent and aggregate interests of the community." Notice what that definition does not require: a faction can be a minority or a majority. What makes a group a faction, in Madison's sense, is not its size but its posture; a shared passion or interest pursued against the rights of others or against the common good of the whole community.

Madison's next move is the one that separates him from most people who complain about faction, then and now. Having named the disease, he refuses the two easiest cures. The first is to "remove the causes" of faction by destroying the liberty that lets people organize around their own opinions and interests in the first place. Madison rejects this instantly and completely: "It could never be more truly said, than of the first remedy, that it is worse than the disease. Liberty is to faction, what air is to fire, an aliment, without which it instantly expires." You cannot eliminate faction without eliminating the freedom that makes faction possible, and a cure that kills the patient to kill the disease is no cure at all. The second easy fix, giving every citizen "the same opinions, the same passions, and the same interests," Madison dismisses as simply impossible: as long as human reason is fallible and free, people will form different opinions, accumulate different amounts of property, and divide into different interests. He traces this directly to something built into free society itself; what he calls "the various and unequal distribution of property," from which landed, mercantile, manufacturing, and other competing interests "grow up of necessity in civilized nations." Faction, for Madison, is not a defect that seeped into republican government by accident. It is a permanent, structural feature of any society where people are free to think, own property, and pursue their own good.

A Direct Answer to Rousseau

Recall Week 4's lesson on Jean-Jacques Rousseau and the general will; Rousseau's claim that a healthy political community could discover and act on a single, unified will aimed at the common good, provided the community stayed small, direct, and free of factions distorting its members' judgment. Madison had read this tradition of thought, and Federalist No. 10 is, among other things, a quiet rejection of it. Rousseau's solution to faction was to prevent it from forming: keep the community small and unified enough that no faction gains a foothold, and trust the assembled people to will the common good directly. Madison's essay argues that this is not a solution at all; it is a description of the exact conditions under which faction becomes most dangerous, not least. If you cannot eliminate the causes of faction, keeping government small and direct does not protect the community from a factious majority; it hands that majority the whole machinery of the state with nothing standing between its passions and its power.

This is why Madison, having ruled out removing the causes of faction, turns to controlling its effects instead, and here his analysis of scale becomes the heart of the essay. A faction that is a minority of the whole is not the deepest danger; "the republican principle," Madison notes, lets the majority out-vote it through ordinary elections. The real danger is a faction that is the majority, because in a government where the majority rules directly, nothing stops that majority from using the law itself to "sacrifice to its ruling passion or interest, both the public good and the rights of other citizens." A pure democracy (Madison's term for "a society consisting of a small number of citizens, who assemble and administer the government in person," exactly the Rousseauian model) offers no defense against this at all. In a small, direct democracy, Madison writes, "there is nothing to check the inducements to sacrifice the weaker party, or an obnoxious individual," which is why such governments "have ever been spectacles of turbulence and contention" and "as short in their lives, as they have been violent in their deaths."

Madison's alternative is a republic (which he defines precisely as "a government in which the scheme of representation takes place") extended over a large and diverse territory. Two features of this extended republic do the work. First, representation itself filters public opinion "through the medium of a chosen body of citizens," whose judgment may (though Madison concedes it may not always) refine and elevate the raw passions of the moment. Second, and more importantly for his argument, sheer size and diversity of interests make it structurally difficult for any single faction to become a stable, coordinated majority at all. "Extend the sphere," Madison writes, "and you take in a greater variety of parties and interests; you make it less probable that a majority of the whole will have a common motive to invade the rights of other citizens; or if such a common motive exists, it will be more difficult for all who feel it to discover their own strength, and to act in unison with each other." A large, commercially and religiously diverse republic does not eliminate faction (nothing can, on Madison's own argument), but it multiplies factions against each other so thoroughly that no single one can easily seize and hold a permanent, oppressive majority. Scale, for Madison, is not a problem the Constitution has to overcome. It is the solution.

You'll read Madison's own words on all of this directly tomorrow, in Federalist No. 10 itself. Read for the definition of faction, the two rejected cures, and the argument for extending the republic's sphere rather than shrinking it, and keep Rousseau in the back of your mind the whole time.
