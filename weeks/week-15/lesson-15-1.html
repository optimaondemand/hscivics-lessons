The Reconstruction Amendments and Plessy v. Ferguson
📖 What You Are Reading Today

Plessy v. Ferguson

U.S. Supreme Court, decided 1896

Reading: How the same Fourteenth Amendment written to guarantee former slaves equal citizenship became, within a single generation, the legal instrument the Supreme Court used to bless state-mandated racial segregation, and the lone justice who said, at the time, that the Court had gotten it wrong.

🎯 By the End of This Lesson You Will Be Able To…
1
State what each of the three Reconstruction Amendments did, and explain specifically why the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause is the provision at stake in this week's cases.
2
Narrate the facts of Homer Plessy's arrest and explain the constitutional question Louisiana's separate-car law forced the Court to answer.
3
State the majority's "separate but equal" holding precisely, explain why it was a serious injustice in practice and not merely in theory, and summarize the reasoning of Justice Harlan's dissent.

Begin with three amendments, ratified in the five years after a war that had killed roughly three-quarters of a million Americans. The Thirteenth Amendment (1865) abolished slavery and involuntary servitude throughout the United States, closing the door on an institution the Constitution had, in 1787, left standing. The Fourteenth Amendment (1868) did several things at once: it made every person born or naturalized in the United States a citizen, of the nation and of the state where they lived; it forbade any state to deprive a person of life, liberty, or property "without due process of law"; and it declared that no state may "deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws." The Fifteenth Amendment (1870) forbade the states or the federal government from denying the right to vote "on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude." Together these are usually called the Reconstruction Amendments, and they were written for an explicit purpose: to make certain that the roughly four million people freed by the Thirteenth Amendment would be citizens in fact, not merely in name, and that no state could reduce them to a second, lesser class of person under the law.

That purpose is the backdrop you need to make sense of what happened next, because the case this lesson centers on is not a case about an ambiguous constitutional question. It is a case about whether the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause actually meant what it said, tested against a law built for the specific purpose of separating citizens by race.

A Train Car in Louisiana

In 1890, the Louisiana legislature passed the Separate Car Act, requiring railroad companies to provide "equal but separate accommodations" for white and Black passengers and making it a crime for a passenger to sit in a car not assigned to his race. The law was resented not only by Black and Creole New Orleans but, for entirely different reasons, by the railroads themselves: running a second, often nearly empty car just to keep the races apart was an expense the East Louisiana Railroad had no wish to bear. A group of Black and Creole professionals in New Orleans, organized as the Citizens' Committee to Test the Constitutionality of the Separate Car Law (the Comité des Citoyens), deliberately arranged a test case to challenge the statute in court, and found a willing partner in the railroad itself. The Committee selected Homer Plessy, one of its own members, a shoemaker who was, by the racial classification of the time, seven-eighths white and one-eighth Black, and who could ordinarily have passed unnoticed in a whites-only car. That was the point: the Committee wanted a case that would force the courts to confront the law's logic directly, not a case complicated by visible appearance.

None of what followed was spontaneous. On June 7, 1892, Plessy purchased a first-class ticket on the East Louisiana Railway and sat in the car reserved for white passengers, exactly as planned. The conductor had been told in advance who Plessy was and knew him by sight; he ordered Plessy to the car reserved for Black passengers regardless of Plessy's appearance, and a private detective, hired for the occasion, stood ready and made the actual arrest once Plessy, following the arrangement, declined to move. This was, in other words, a coordinated test case staged jointly by the Committee, its chosen defendant, and the railroad running the train, engineered precisely so the resulting arrest would present the courts with the cleanest possible version of the constitutional question. The case against him went to trial before Judge John H. Ferguson of the Orleans Parish criminal court, who ruled that Louisiana could constitutionally regulate railway accommodations within its own borders. Plessy appealed, and the case, Plessy v. Ferguson, reached the United States Supreme Court in 1896.

"Separate but Equal"

The Court ruled against Plessy, 7 to 1. Writing for the majority, Justice Henry Billings Brown held that Louisiana's law did not violate the Fourteenth Amendment. His reasoning ran roughly as follows: the Fourteenth Amendment guaranteed the equality of citizens before the law, but it did not, in his view, abolish distinctions based on color, nor was it intended to force "commingling of the two races" in ways "unsatisfactory to either." A state law requiring separate facilities was constitutional, the majority held, so long as the separate facilities provided were substantially equal. This is the doctrine now known as separate but equal: state-mandated racial segregation does not, by itself, violate the Equal Protection Clause, provided the separated facilities are nominally equivalent.

Justice Brown went further, and this part of the opinion deserves to be read as plainly as it was written, because it shows the majority's reasoning at its weakest. Brown wrote that if Black passengers felt the Separate Car Act stamped them with a "badge of inferiority," that feeling arose "solely because the colored race chooses to put that construction upon it"; that is, the majority located the injury not in the law itself but in the feelings of the people the law targeted. He also argued, by analogy, that laws requiring separate schools for white and Black children were already widely accepted "even by courts of States where the political rights of the colored race have been longest and most earnestly enforced," treating existing segregation as evidence of its own legitimacy.

State this plainly, because softening it would be dishonest: in practice, "separate" facilities across the American South were almost never actually equal. Black train cars, schools, courthouses, and public accommodations were, with very rare exception, underfunded, inferior, and deliberately built to communicate and enforce a racial hierarchy, not merely to keep the races physically apart. Plessy did not create Southern segregation, which was already spreading rapidly through state law by the 1890s, but it gave that segregation the Supreme Court's constitutional blessing. For the next fifty-eight years, Plessy stood as the governing precedent that let states construct and defend an entire legal architecture of racial separation (the system historians call Jim Crow) in schools, transportation, housing, voting, and public life generally, all while claiming compliance with the very Equal Protection Clause written to prevent exactly this outcome.

Harlan's Dissent

One justice disagreed, and disagreed in terms that read, from this distance, as remarkably clear-sighted. Justice John Marshall Harlan, a former slaveholder from Kentucky who had come to oppose segregation as a matter of constitutional principle, wrote alone in dissent. Harlan argued that the Louisiana law's actual purpose and effect was not neutral separation but the assertion of racial dominance: "Every one knows that the statute in question had its origin in the purpose... to exclude colored people from coaches occupied by or assigned to white persons," he wrote, and no one would mistake its purpose if the races were reversed. He rejected the majority's premise that a law could treat citizens differently by race and still satisfy equal protection merely because the facilities were labeled equal. Most famously, Harlan wrote that "our Constitution is color-blind, and neither knows nor tolerates classes among citizens," and warned that the majority's decision would prove "quite as pernicious as the decision made by this tribunal in the Dred Scott case"; a direct, pointed comparison to the Court's other great constitutional failure. Harlan's dissent commanded no majority in 1896. It would take fifty-eight years, and a different Court, for his reasoning to become the law of the land.
