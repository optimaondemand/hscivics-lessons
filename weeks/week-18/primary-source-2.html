📖 Context Before You Read

Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn spent eight years as a prisoner in the Soviet forced-labor camp system after making a critical remark about Stalin in a private letter during World War II. After his release, he spent years secretly gathering testimony from hundreds of fellow former prisoners, weaving their accounts together with his own experience into a work he could not publish inside the Soviet Union. The Gulag Archipelago circulated first in the West beginning in 1973, smuggled out of the country at serious risk to Solzhenitsyn and those who helped him; he was stripped of his Soviet citizenship and expelled from the country the following year. The work remains under copyright, so this lesson presents its content in paraphrase and historical summary rather than extended quotation. Read for what the work documents, and why the act of documenting it, against a state actively trying to prevent exactly that, was itself a civic act of real consequence.

Solzhenitsyn's work documents the scale and mechanics of the Soviet forced-labor camp system across the decades of its operation: the arrest procedures, often based on denunciations or arbitrary quotas rather than genuine evidence of wrongdoing; the interrogation methods used to extract confessions; the transport of prisoners by rail across enormous distances to remote camps; and the forced labor, chronic hunger, and extreme cold that killed a substantial proportion of those sent to the camps over the system's history. He organized these accounts not as a single memoir but as a synthesis of testimony gathered from a wide network of fellow survivors, explicitly framing the work as an act of historical record-keeping the Soviet state itself refused to permit.

Solzhenitsyn was direct about his purpose: he intended the work to make it impossible, going forward, for the scale of the Gulag system to be minimized, denied, or forgotten, either by the Soviet state or by outside observers inclined to give that state the benefit of the doubt. He treated the act of testimony (ordinary people's accounts of what had actually happened to them) as a form of evidence a totalitarian state's official narrative could not ultimately withstand, once that testimony reached an audience beyond the state's control. In one of the book's most widely cited passages, he draws the lesson away from any single ideology or nation and toward every reader individually: "Gradually it was disclosed to me that the line separating good and evil passes not through states, nor between classes, nor between political parties either, but right through every human heart, and through all human hearts."

Historical summary and paraphrase of Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn, The Gulag Archipelago (first published 1973), with one short, widely reproduced direct quotation (Part I, ch. 2). The work remains under copyright; beyond this single well-known passage, longer verbatim excerpting is not appropriate here, so the rest is presented in historical summary and paraphrase.
💡 Reading Jones and Solzhenitsyn Together

Both of this week's primary sources are, at bottom, about the same thing: an individual risking real personal cost to tell the truth about a totalitarian state's treatment of its own people, against that state's active effort to prevent exactly that disclosure. Jones did it as an outside journalist walking into a famine zone in 1933; Solzhenitsyn did it as a former prisoner secretly compiling testimony decades later. Hold both examples in mind for this week's VR Activity and the Module 2 Outro that follows; they are two versions of the same civic act this course has asked you to value all semester: telling the truth about power, even when power would prefer silence.
