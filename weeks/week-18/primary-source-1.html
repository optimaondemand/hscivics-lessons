Reporting on the Soviet famine, 1933
Primary Source 1
Gareth Jones, "Famine Rules Russia"
📖 Context Before You Read

Gareth Jones was a young Welsh journalist who, in March 1933, defied Soviet travel restrictions to walk unaccompanied through villages in Ukraine and the surrounding countryside, at the height of what is now known as the Holodomor, a catastrophic famine that killed millions in Soviet Ukraine and other grain-producing regions in 1932 and 1933. Historians continue to debate exactly how much of the famine's severity resulted from deliberate policy rather than mismanagement and drought, but the core facts are not seriously disputed: the Soviet state's forced collectivization of agriculture, punishing grain-requisition quotas set from Moscow, and its refusal to permit outside aid or even to acknowledge the famine's existence, turned an agricultural crisis into a mass death event, largely concealed from the outside world at the time. Most Western journalists stationed in Moscow, working under Soviet censorship and dependent on official access to do their jobs, either downplayed or actively denied Jones's account; some famously dismissed it in print. Time and later archival research vindicated Jones's reporting.

"I tramped through a number of villages in the snow of March. I saw children with swollen bellies. I slept in peasants['] huts, sometimes nine of us in one room. I talked to every peasant I met, and the general conclusion I draw is that the present state of Russian agriculture is already catastrophic but that in a year['s] time its condition will have worsened tenfold. What did the peasants say? There was one cry which resounded everywhere I went and that was: 'There is no bread.' The other sentence, which [was] as the leitmotiv of my Russian visit[,] was: 'All are swollen.' Even within a few miles of Moscow there is no bread left. As I was going through the countryside in that district I chatted to several women who were trudging with empty sacks towards Moscow. They all said: 'It is terrible. We have no bread. We have to go all the way to Moscow to get bread.'"

Gareth Jones, "Famine Rules Russia," The London Evening Standard, March 31, 1933. Public domain (author died 1935; UK copyright term expired 2005).
💡 Why One Reporter's Account Matters

Jones was not a historian working decades later with archives open to him. He was a young journalist walking into a famine zone the Soviet state was actively trying to hide, at real personal risk, and telling the truth about what he found while nearly everyone with easier access to publish chose not to. Hold that fact in mind for this week's Critical Questions and for Wednesday's lesson, which returns to the Soviet state's pattern of denying and concealing the human cost of its own policies.
