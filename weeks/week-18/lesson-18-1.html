Lesson 18.1
Communism as a Governmental and Economic System
📖 What You Are Reading Today

Topic: This course has spent seventeen weeks studying a single, sustained argument: that legitimate government power is power distributed, limited, and made answerable to the people it governs. This week asks what happens when a government rejects that argument outright. We begin with communism (not as a caricature, but as an actual body of political and economic theory), and then trace the specific historical distance between that theory and the one-party state the Soviet Union built in its name.

🎯 By the End of This Lesson You Will Be Able To…
1
State the core claims of Marxist theory (class struggle, the abolition of private property in the means of production, and the theoretical goal of a classless society) accurately and in their own terms.
2
Explain how the Soviet one-party state and command economy concentrated political and economic power in a single party apparatus.
3
Identify, by name, which structural checks studied all semester (separation of powers, an independent judiciary, protected civil liberties) had no counterpart in the Soviet system, and explain why their absence matters.

Begin with the theory, on its own terms, before any government ever claimed to build it. Communism, as a body of political and economic thought, originates chiefly with the German philosopher and economist Karl Marx, writing together with Friedrich Engels in the mid-nineteenth century. Marx looked at industrializing Europe and saw not a story of general progress but a story of class struggle: history, in his account, is driven by conflict between those who own the means of production (land, factories, capital), and those who do not and must sell their labor to survive. Marx called the first group the bourgeoisie and the second the proletariat, and he argued that this conflict was not incidental to capitalism but built into its structure: the owning class profits precisely by paying labor less than the value labor creates.

Marx's proposed solution was not merely to regulate this arrangement but to abolish its foundation: the private ownership of the means of production. If land, factories, and capital were held in common rather than owned privately, he argued, the underlying conflict between owner and worker would dissolve, because the category of "owner extracting profit from worker" would no longer exist. The theoretical endpoint of this process, in Marx's own writing, was a classless society; a world in which the state itself, having no ruling class interest left to protect, would eventually become unnecessary and "wither away." It is worth sitting with that last detail, because it will matter enormously later in this lesson: Marx's own theory promised the eventual disappearance of centralized state power, not its concentration.

From Theory to the Soviet State

What actually happened in Russia after the Bolshevik Revolution of 1917, and hardened under Joseph Stalin from the late 1920s onward, was something the theory itself had not really specified and, on its own terms, had not particularly wanted: a single political party, the Communist Party of the Soviet Union, claiming exclusive and permanent authority to govern in the name of the proletariat, indefinitely, with no scheduled transfer of power and no legal path for any rival party to contest it. Political theorists have a name for a state organized this way: a one-party state, a system in which one party controls the government by law or by force and no competing party is permitted to exist, campaign, or hold office.

The Soviet economy was reorganized to match. Private ownership of major industry, agriculture, and enterprise was abolished, and in its place the state built a command economy: a system in which centralized state planners, not prices set by independent buyers and sellers, decide what is produced, in what quantity, and at what cost. Soviet planning agencies set five-year targets for steel, grain, tractors, and housing from Moscow, for the entire union, without the price signals a market economy uses to tell producers what is actually needed or wanted. The state, in other words, did not merely govern the Soviet Union's politics; it owned and directed its economy as well.

Put those two facts together and you arrive at the concept this lesson exists to teach: totalitarianism, a system of government that seeks to control not merely the political process but the whole of a society's economic, cultural, and personal life, typically through a single party, an official ideology, and the machinery of state coercion. The word matters because it names something more total than ordinary authoritarian rule. An authoritarian government might suppress political opposition while leaving economic and private life largely alone. A totalitarian government does not recognize a private sphere at all; political power and economic power are fused in the same hands, and there is, in principle, no corner of a citizen's life the state does not claim authority to reach.

Reading the Soviet State Against the American Constitution

You have spent this entire course building a specific vocabulary for evaluating governments, and this is the moment to use every word of it deliberately, side by side. Begin with separation of powers; the division of governmental authority among distinct legislative, executive, and judicial branches, each checking the others, that you studied at length in Module 1's study of Article I and the Constitution's later articles. The Soviet Union had no structural counterpart to this at all. The Communist Party's Politburo and General Secretary combined what a constitutional government would separate: the power to write law, the power to enforce it, and, as the next point shows, the power to interpret and apply it as well.

Continue to judicial independence; the principle, embedded in Article III of the Constitution and reinforced by the lifetime tenure of federal judges, that courts must be able to rule against the government itself without fear of retaliation. Soviet courts were not independent in this sense. Judges served at the Party's pleasure, and the most consequential prosecutions of the Stalin era; the show trials of the 1930s, in which defendants confessed to fantastic crimes against the state after coerced interrogation, and were convicted in proceedings staged for their political effect rather than their legal accuracy; were directed political theater, not the disinterested administration of law you studied in this course's unit on the American judiciary.

Finally, consider the civil liberties you traced through the Bill of Rights and its landmark cases: freedom of speech and of the press, freedom of religion, protection against arbitrary search, seizure, and detention, due process before the state can take life, liberty, or property. The Soviet constitution of 1936, on paper, listed rights that sound similar to these. In practice, none of them constrained the Party, because no independent court, no free press, and no competing political power existed to enforce them against the state that had written them. A right unenforceable against the government that grants it is not a constitutional constraint in the sense this course has used that term all semester; it is a promise the state can suspend at will, precisely because nothing outside the state has the standing or the power to hold it to its word.

This comparison is the whole point of the lesson, so state it plainly. The American constitutional structure you have studied since Week 1 rests on a wager: that no single person or party can be trusted with unchecked power, so power itself must be divided, checked, and made answerable to courts, elections, and a written law that binds the government along with the governed. The Soviet system rejected that wager at every point where the American Constitution makes it. It fused legislative, executive, and judicial power in one party; it eliminated the independent judiciary that might have checked that party's abuses; and it left civil liberties dependent entirely on the goodwill of the same authority those liberties were supposed to restrain. What that fusion of unchecked power actually produced, in the lived experience of Soviet citizens, is where this week's study turns next.
