The Least Dangerous Branch, and Aristotle on Constitutions That Decay
📖 What You Are Reading Today

Topic: Hamilton's case for an independent judiciary; written fifteen years before any American court actually struck down a federal law, but already arguing for the power to do so. Then Aristotle, writing over two thousand years earlier, on why constitutions decay, and what kind of failure the framers were most afraid of repeating.

Alexander Hamilton did not live to see the argument of Federalist No. 78 vindicated in practice. He wrote the essay in 1788, defending the proposed judiciary's design against critics who worried that unelected judges, serving for life, posed a threat to republican self-government. It was not until 1803, in Marbury v. Madison, that the Supreme Court actually exercised the power Hamilton describes here; striking down an act of Congress as unconstitutional, establishing in practice what is now called judicial review, the power of courts to declare a law void when it conflicts with the Constitution. Chief Justice John Marshall's opinion in that case is often treated as the founding moment of American judicial review. But the theoretical foundation for that power was already fully articulated fifteen years earlier, in the essay you'll read tomorrow. Reading No. 78 is, in a sense, reading the argument for judicial review before anyone had actually used it.

Neither the Sword Nor the Purse

Hamilton's essay opens by comparing the three branches of the proposed government according to what each one actually controls. The executive, he observes, "holds the sword of the community": command of the military and the enforcement power of the state. The legislature "commands the purse": the power to tax, spend, and write the laws that govern every citizen's rights and duties. The judiciary, by contrast, has "no influence over either the sword or the purse; no direction either of the strength or of the wealth of the society." It can order nothing enforced and appropriate nothing spent. Hamilton's famous formulation is that the judiciary has "neither Force nor Will, but merely judgment," and must depend on the executive branch even to carry out its own rulings. From this comparison he draws his central claim: of the three departments, the judiciary "will always be the least dangerous to the political rights of the constitution," precisely because it is structurally the weakest, least equipped to act aggressively against the other branches or against the people.

This claim does real argumentative work. Hamilton's critics worried that unelected judges serving for life were a standing danger to republican liberty; an unaccountable aristocracy in robes. Hamilton's response is not to deny that judges hold significant power, but to reframe the comparison: whatever risk an independent judiciary poses, it is a smaller risk than either an executive with unchecked command of force or a legislature with unchecked command of money and lawmaking. The real danger, Hamilton argues, would come not from judicial independence itself but from the judiciary losing that independence and becoming an extension of one of the other, genuinely dangerous branches.

Life Tenure and Secure Pay, as a Safeguard

Because the judiciary is naturally the weakest branch, Hamilton argues, it needs the strongest possible institutional protection to keep it independent of the other two. This is his case for life tenure; judges serving "during good behaviour," removable only for genuine misconduct, not at the pleasure of whoever appointed them or whoever currently controls Congress. If judges held office only for a fixed term, or served at the will of the president or Congress, Hamilton warns, they would face constant pressure to rule in ways that pleased whichever branch controlled their reappointment or their livelihood; exactly the kind of dependency that would destroy the judiciary's usefulness as a check on the other branches. He extends this logic to judicial salaries as well: a legislature that could reduce a judge's pay at will would hold real leverage over that judge's decisions, so the Constitution guards judicial compensation from being diminished during a judge's time in office. Life tenure and secure pay are not perks, in Hamilton's argument; they are the specific mechanisms that let a structurally weak branch resist pressure from the two genuinely powerful ones.

Hamilton then makes the argument for judicial review itself, years before any court used it. If the Constitution is the fundamental law, superior to any ordinary statute Congress might pass, then a law that conflicts with the Constitution cannot be valid law at all, and someone must have the authority to say so in a specific case. Hamilton argues this authority naturally belongs to the courts, whose whole function is interpreting law and applying it to specific cases. This does not mean, he is careful to add, that the judiciary is somehow superior to the legislature; it means that the Constitution (the people's fundamental law) is superior to both, and where an ordinary statute conflicts with it, judges are bound to follow the higher law. This is precisely the reasoning John Marshall would echo, nearly word for word in spirit, when the Supreme Court actually struck down a federal statute for the first time in 1803.

Aristotle's Map of Constitutions

Turn now to a much older text, one both Hamilton and Madison had studied closely: Aristotle's Politics, written in Athens in the fourth century BC as a systematic study of how cities are actually governed and how they ought to be. In Book III and carried forward into Books IV and V, Aristotle lays out a typology of constitutions that organizes them along two axes: how many people rule (one, few, or many), and whether that rule serves the common good or only the private advantage of the rulers.

Rule by one, aiming at the common good, Aristotle calls monarchy; rule by one aiming only at the ruler's private advantage he calls tyranny; monarchy's corrupt twin. Rule by a few, aiming at the common good, he calls aristocracy (literally, from the Greek, "rule of the best"); rule by a few aiming only at their own advantage he calls oligarchy, rule by and for the wealthy few. Rule by the many, aiming at the common good, Aristotle calls polity; a term distinct from simple "democracy" in his usage; rule by the many aiming only at the advantage of the poor majority, at the expense of the whole community's good, he calls, in his own more critical usage, a corrupted democracy, closer to what we might call mob rule. Six constitutions in all, arranged in three matched pairs, each pair distinguished not by who rules but by whether that rule serves everyone or only the rulers themselves.

This typology is not just a classification exercise. It carries a warning built into its structure: every one of the three "good" forms sits directly beside a corrupted version of itself, separated only by whether power is used for the common good or turned toward private or factional advantage. Aristotle's point is that no single form of government is safe simply by virtue of its structure; a monarchy can decay into tyranny, an aristocracy into oligarchy, a polity into mob rule, if the persons or groups holding power stop governing for the whole community and start governing for themselves.

Why Constitutions Actually Fall Apart

In Book V, Aristotle turns from classifying constitutions to explaining what causes them to change or collapse, and his answer centers on a single recurring cause: a perceived mismatch between political power and honor on one side, and merit or contribution on the other. Revolutions and constitutional collapse, Aristotle argues, generally arise "from a desire of equality," when people who consider themselves equal to others find themselves receiving less than an equal share of office, honor, or advantage, or, just as dangerously, from a desire for superiority, when people who consider themselves superior in merit or contribution find themselves treated as merely equal, or worse, subordinate. Both oligarchies and democracies, in Aristotle's analysis, are vulnerable to this same underlying dynamic: whichever group holds power tends, over time, to press its advantage further than justice warrants, provoking the resentment of those left out, until the excluded group's sense of injustice becomes strong enough to overturn the existing order, often replacing one imbalance with its opposite rather than achieving genuine balance.

Notice how close this is to a concern running through Hamilton's essays and Madison's alike. Aristotle worried that a democracy's majority, left unchecked, would eventually govern for its own advantage rather than the whole community's good, tipping a polity into mob rule; he worried, in the same breath, that an oligarchy's wealthy few would do exactly the same thing in the other direction, tipping an aristocracy into naked rule by and for the rich. The founders inherited this same worry in a different vocabulary. Madison's fear of majority faction in Federalist No. 10 is, in substance, Aristotle's fear of democracy decaying into mob rule. Hamilton's insistence, in No. 78, on an independent judiciary answerable to the Constitution rather than to any momentary majority or any single powerful branch is a structural answer to the same underlying problem Aristotle diagnosed: unchecked power, whether held by one, by a few, or by the many, tends over time to bend government away from the common good and toward the advantage of whoever holds it. Checks and balances, separation of powers, and an independent judiciary are not just clever engineering. They are the founders' considered response to a pattern of constitutional decay that Aristotle had already mapped out more than two thousand years before Philadelphia.
