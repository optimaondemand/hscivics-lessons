Lesson 8.1
Hamilton and the Problem of Choosing a President
📖 What You Are Reading Today

The Federalist, No. 68

Alexander Hamilton, 1788

Reading: Why the Constitutional Convention rejected both a direct popular vote and a legislative appointment for choosing the president, and built instead an unusual middle institution (the Electoral College) to solve a problem no republic before America had solved at this scale: how do you select a single chief executive for an enormous, dispersed country whose citizens cannot possibly know every candidate personally?

🎯 By the End of This Lesson You Will Be Able To…
1
State the two methods of selecting a chief executive that the Constitutional Convention rejected, and explain the specific danger each one posed.
2
Explain how the Electoral College, as Hamilton describes it in Federalist No. 68, was designed to combine popular input with deliberative judgment while resisting foreign influence and cabal.
3
Describe, as a matter of historical fact, how the practical operation of the Electoral College has changed since 1787; particularly the rise of electors pledged to a party ticket.

Of all the difficult questions facing the delegates in Philadelphia in the summer of 1787, few caused more disagreement, or were settled later in the convention, than this one: how should the United States choose its chief executive? The delegates agreed early that the new government needed a single, energetic executive rather than a committee; a lesson learned from the weak, plural executive of the Articles of Confederation period. But how to select that executive divided them for most of the summer. Two options came up again and again, and the convention eventually rejected both.

The first option was direct popular election; letting the whole voting citizenry of the United States cast ballots for president, the winner determined by national popular vote. This sounds, to modern ears, like the obviously democratic choice. But the delegates in 1787 faced a practical problem almost impossible to appreciate from a media-saturated twenty-first century vantage point: there was no way for a voter in Georgia to know anything reliable about a candidate from Massachusetts, or a voter in New Hampshire to evaluate a candidate from South Carolina. No national newspapers circulated widely enough to inform the whole country about every plausible candidate's character and record. Communication across thirteen states took days or weeks. Many delegates worried that a national popular election, under those conditions, would not reward the most qualified candidate but the most demagogue; a word built from the Greek demos (the people) and agogos (leading), meaning a leader who wins power by flattering popular passions and prejudices rather than by genuine merit. Worse, with no way for ordinary citizens across such distances to verify a candidate's fitness for office, the door stood open to manipulation by whoever could most effectively spread a compelling story, true or false, faster than it could be checked.

The second option was selection by the national legislature; letting Congress choose the president, the way many state legislatures chose their governors, and the way parliamentary systems would later choose prime ministers. This solved the information problem: members of Congress, deliberating together in one place, could genuinely evaluate candidates' records and qualifications. But it created a different and, to the framers, more dangerous problem. If the president depended entirely on Congress for his selection and reappointment, he would have every incentive to become Congress's creature; a dependent, obliging executive who curried favor with legislators to secure his continuation in office, rather than exercising independent judgment. This would collapse the separation of powers that the framers considered essential to preventing any single branch from accumulating dangerous, unchecked authority. An executive who serves at the legislature's pleasure is not really a separate branch of government at all; he is an extension of it.

Hamilton's Middle Path

Into this impasse Alexander Hamilton, writing as Publius in Federalist No. 68, defended the convention's actual solution: neither direct popular election nor legislative selection, but a specially created body of presidential electors, chosen by whatever method each state's own legislature decided to use, who would assemble separately within their own states, deliberate, and cast votes for president. Hamilton opens the essay with unusual confidence for a document written to win over skeptics: of all the parts of the proposed Constitution, he claims, "the mode of appointment of the chief magistrate" has drawn less criticism than almost anything else in the whole plan, and he goes further than the critics' grudging approval, arguing that the system is not merely well-guarded but positively excellent.

Hamilton lays out exactly what the Electoral College was built to achieve, and it is worth reading his own list of goals before you read the essay itself tomorrow. First, "the sense of the people should operate in the choice"; the electors would be chosen by the people (however each state arranged that choice), not appointed by some permanent aristocratic body detached from popular consent. Second, the choice should be made "by men most capable of analyzing the qualities adapted to the station"; a small deliberative body, Hamilton argues, is simply better positioned to investigate a candidate's fitness than the mass of citizens voting all at once with imperfect information. Third, the system should minimize "tumult and disorder": by having electors meet separately in their own states rather than converging on one another in a single assembly, no single mob-like gathering could sweep electors along on a wave of collective passion the way, Hamilton implies, a national convention or a single legislative chamber might. And fourth (this is where Hamilton's argument becomes most concerned with a danger the framers took extremely seriously) the system should resist "cabal, intrigue, and corruption," especially the risk that a foreign power might try to install "a creature of their own" as head of the American government. Independent electors, dispersed across thirteen states, meeting briefly and then dissolving, presented nothing like the standing, bribable target that a small permanent council or a sitting legislature would have offered a foreign agent with resources and time.

Notice the structural logic running through all four goals. The Electoral College was designed as neither a pure democratic instrument nor an aristocratic one, but a deliberately temporary, decentralized, independent institution; created fresh for each election, dissolved immediately after, accountable to the people through its selection but insulated, once chosen, from ongoing pressure by Congress, by the sitting president, or by any faction with the patience to corrupt a permanent body over time. Hamilton predicts the result will be, in his words, "a constant probability of seeing the station filled by characters preeminent for ability and virtue"; not a guarantee, but a system whose incentive structure tilts toward competence rather than mere popularity.

A System That Has Changed in Practice

One historical fact is worth stating plainly before you read Hamilton's own words: the Electoral College Hamilton describes in 1788 is not, in its everyday operation, identical to the Electoral College of later American history. Hamilton imagined electors as independent deliberators; men who would meet, discuss, and exercise their own judgment about who was best qualified, largely free of any obligation to vote a particular way. Within a few decades of the founding, however, the rise of organized political parties changed how electors were chosen and how they behaved once chosen. By the early nineteenth century, states had moved toward selecting electors pledged in advance to vote for a specific party's ticket, and that practice has held, with local variation, ever since. Today's electors are, in nearly every case, party loyalists selected precisely because they are expected to cast their vote for their party's candidate, not independent deliberators exercising the kind of case-by-case judgment Hamilton described. This is a factual description of how the institution's practical operation evolved over time from its original constitutional design; a historical development worth understanding clearly, distinct from any contested argument about whether the modern system should be kept, reformed, or replaced.

You'll read Hamilton's own words on all of this directly tomorrow, in Federalist No. 68 itself. Read for his defense of an intermediate body of electors, his account of what makes it resistant to corruption, and his prediction about the caliber of person it would tend to elevate to the presidency.
