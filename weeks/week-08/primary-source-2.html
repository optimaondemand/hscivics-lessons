Aristotle, Politics, Books IV–V
📖 Context Before You Read

Aristotle's Politics is a different work from the Constitution of Athens you read in Week 1; where that earlier text narrated the specific history of one city's government, the Politics is a general theoretical study, based on Aristotle's comparative survey of dozens of Greek constitutions, of what makes any constitution stable, just, or prone to collapse. Books IV and V, composed in Athens in the fourth century BC, extend the six-part typology of constitutions introduced earlier in the work. Read for two arguments: what Aristotle thinks actually causes revolutions (Book V), and why he thinks a large middle class, not a virtuous king or a righteous majority, is a constitution's real safeguard (Book IV).

"Democracies have arisen from supposing that those who are equal in one thing are so in every other circumstance; as, because they are equal in liberty, they are equal in everything else; and oligarchies, from supposing that those who are unequal in one thing are unequal in all... Hence it follows, that those who in some respects are equal with others think it right to endeavour to partake of an equality with them in everything; and those who are superior to others endeavour to get still more: and it is this more which is the inequality... and, upon this account, when either party has not that share in the administration which answers to his expectations, he becomes seditious."

"In every city the people are divided into three sorts; the very rich, the very poor, and those who are between them. If this is universally admitted, that the mean is best, it is evident that even in point of fortune mediocrity is to be preferred... A city composed of such men [only the very rich and the very poor] must therefore consist of slaves and masters, not freemen... We ought to consider as a proof of what I now advance, that the best lawgivers themselves were those in the middle rank of life, amongst whom was Solon, as is evident from his poems, and Lycurgus... But even in those [democracies] when there is a want of a proper number of men of middling fortune, the poor extend their power too far, abuses arise, and the government is soon at an end."

Aristotle, Politics, Book V, ch. I, and Book IV, ch. XI, trans. William Ellis (London: J.M. Dent, 1912). Public domain.
💡 Why This Counts as a Primary Source

Aristotle's own text, even summarized here, is the primary evidence for his theory; the same way Hamilton's essay, not a textbook description of it, is the primary evidence for Hamilton's argument. Notice the shared concern running through both of this week's sources: Hamilton worries about a judiciary losing its independence to a more powerful branch; Aristotle worries about any form of rule, one, few, or many, losing sight of the common good once nothing checks it. Read them against each other, not just side by side.
