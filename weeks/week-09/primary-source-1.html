📖 Context Before You Read

This is the actual ratified text of Article I, Sections 1 through 3; the sections that establish Congress and its two chambers. Read it slowly. It is dense, procedural, and written in the legal style of 1787, but every clause below answers a specific design question the Convention had just spent months arguing about. Read as an annotated guide this time, not for a formal Critical Questions assessment: pause after each numbered clause and ask yourself what problem it solves.

Section 1. "All legislative Powers herein granted shall be vested in a Congress of the United States, which shall consist of a Senate and House of Representatives."

Annotation: The bicameral structure, stated in one sentence. Every other clause in Article I exists to define these two bodies and how they relate.

Section 2. "The House of Representatives shall be composed of Members chosen every second Year by the People of the several States… No Person shall be a Representative who shall not have attained to the Age of twenty five Years, and been seven Years a Citizen of the United States, and who shall not, when elected, be an Inhabitant of that State in which he shall be chosen… The House of Representatives shall chuse their Speaker and other Officers; and shall have the sole Power of Impeachment."

Annotation: Two-year terms, direct election "by the People," a lower age and citizenship threshold than the Senate. The House also holds the exclusive power to impeach (to formally accuse a federal officer of wrongdoing), reflecting its closer link to the people it represents.

Section 3. "The Senate of the United States shall be composed of two Senators from each State, chosen by the Legislature thereof, for six Years… Immediately after they shall be assembled in Consequence of the first Election, they shall be divided as equally as may be into three Classes… so that one-third may be chosen every second Year… No Person shall be a Senator who shall not have attained to the Age of thirty Years, and been nine Years a Citizen of the United States… The Senate shall have the sole Power to try all Impeachments… and no Person shall be convicted without the Concurrence of two-thirds of the Members present."

Annotation: Equal representation per state (two Senators each, regardless of population; the small-state half of the Great Compromise), six-year terms with only a third of the seats turning over at a time, higher age and citizenship thresholds, and original election by state legislatures rather than the voters. The Senate alone tries impeachments the House brings, and conviction requires a two-thirds vote: a deliberately high bar.

💡 Check Your Understanding

Answer the following for yourself or in class discussion. These are ungraded; there is no single correct answer to most of them, and no rubric attached. Their purpose is to make sure the structure above has actually landed before you move to Thursday's reading on enumerated powers.

1
The House and Senate divide impeachment power between them; the House accuses, the Senate tries the case. Why might the framers have wanted two different bodies involved in removing a federal officer, rather than giving one chamber the whole process?
2
Only one-third of the Senate is up for election in any given cycle, while the entire House is up for election every two years. What is gained, and what is lost, by never letting the whole Senate turn over at once?
3
If you had been a delegate from a small state at the Convention, which feature of Section 3 would you have insisted on keeping, no matter what else was negotiated away? Why?
