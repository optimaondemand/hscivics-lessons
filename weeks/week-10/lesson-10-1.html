Lesson 10.1
The President's Job Description: Article II and the Take Care Clause
📖 What You Are Reading Today

Constitution of the United States, Article II

Ratified 1788

Reading: Where the Constitution turns from the body that writes the nation's laws to the office that carries them out, and where a single clause, easy to read past, quietly defines the difference between the two.

🎯 By the End of This Lesson You Will Be Able To…
1
State the three constitutional qualifications for the presidency and explain the Take Care Clause as the textual heart of the distinction between lawmaking and policymaking.
2
Identify at least four of the President's enumerated powers under Article II, including the commander-in-chief power, the treaty power, the appointment power, and the duty to report to Congress.
3
Name the four amendments that later modified the presidency, and state in one sentence what each one changed.

Article I took the framers seven sections to build a bicameral Congress, apportion its two chambers, and list, clause by clause, everything that body may do. Article II, by contrast, is short. It creates a single office, gives it a handful of named powers, and then compresses the entire theory of what that office is for into one sentence buried in its third section. That compression is not an accident of drafting. It reflects a deliberate choice about what kind of power the presidency was meant to hold, and it is the key to this week's central distinction: the difference between lawmaking, which the Constitution assigns to Congress, and policymaking, the shaping of how the nation is actually governed day to day, which the presidency exercises in a narrower, more contested way.

Article II, Section 1 opens by vesting "the executive Power" in a President of the United States, and immediately sets three qualifications for the office: a President must be a natural-born citizen (a citizen at birth, rather than by later naturalization), at least thirty-five years old, and a resident within the United States for at least fourteen years. Compare these to the Senate's thresholds of thirty years old and nine years of citizenship, which you read in Week 9; the presidency's bar is set higher still, on both age and years of residency, reflecting the framers' judgment that the nation's single chief executive should bring more seasoning to the office than a member of its more numerous legislature.

The Sentence That Defines the Office

Section 3 of Article II lists several presidential duties in a single long sentence (reporting to Congress on the state of the union, recommending measures Congress "shall judge necessary and expedient," receiving ambassadors from foreign nations), and then arrives at the clause this lesson is built around: the President "shall take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed." This is the Take Care Clause, and it states, as plainly as the Constitution states anything, what the executive branch's constitutional job actually is. Congress writes the law. The President's assigned task is to see that the law, once written, is carried out faithfully; administered, enforced, and applied as Congress wrote it, not rewritten according to the President's own preferences.

That is the theory. In practice, the line between executing a law and shaping what that law actually does in the world is far less crisp than the Take Care Clause's language suggests, and this course will not pretend otherwise. A law rarely specifies every detail of its own enforcement; someone has to decide how strictly to enforce it, which violations to prioritize, and how to interpret ambiguous language, and the executive branch makes exactly those decisions every day through the federal agencies under the President's direction. Modern presidents also shape policy more directly still: by proposing legislation for Congress to take up (Section 3's "recommend to their Consideration such Measures" is the textual seed of every presidential legislative agenda since), and by issuing executive orders; formal directives to federal agencies on how to carry out existing law and manage the executive branch's own operations, which do not create new law but can substantially shape how existing law is applied. None of this is a violation of Article II. It is the space the Constitution leaves, deliberately or not, between the clean textual distinction of "Congress makes law, the President executes it" and the messier reality of governing a modern nation. Holding both truths at once (the constitutional design and its practical elasticity) is the intellectual task this week asks of you.

The Enumerated Powers of Article II

Beyond the Take Care Clause, Article II grants the President a specific, named set of powers, considerably shorter than Congress's list in Article I, Section 8. The President is commander-in-chief of the Army, Navy, and, when called into federal service, the state militias; command of the armed forces, though recall from Week 9 that only Congress may formally declare war. The President may make treaties with foreign nations, but, echoing the Senate's foreign-policy role you studied last week, only "by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate," and only if two-thirds of Senators present concur. The President appoints ambassadors, federal judges, and other high officers of the United States, again "by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate," which in practice means Senate confirmation by simple majority for most offices. The President must periodically give Congress information on the state of the union (the origin of the modern State of the Union address) and receives ambassadors and other public ministers from foreign governments, making the presidency, by constitutional design, the nation's single point of contact in its formal diplomatic relationships.

Notice the pattern running through nearly every one of these powers: the President acts, but rarely alone. Treaties require the Senate's two-thirds consent; appointments require the Senate's confirmation; command of the military does not include the power to declare the war it might be called to fight. This is not incidental. It is the same logic of divided, checked authority that shaped Article I, applied now to the executive.

Four Amendments That Later Reshaped the Office

Article II as ratified in 1788 has since been modified by four amendments, each worth knowing by name and function, even though this lesson will not examine any of them in depth. The Twelfth Amendment (1804) rewrote the Electoral College's voting procedure after the election of 1800 produced a tie-breaking crisis, requiring electors to cast separate ballots for President and Vice President rather than one ballot for each. The Twentieth Amendment (1933) moved the presidential inauguration date from March 4 to January 20, shortening the "lame duck" period between a November election and the new administration taking office. The Twenty-Second Amendment (1951) formally limited a President to two elected terms, codifying a two-term norm that every President from Washington through Franklin Roosevelt had followed voluntarily, until Roosevelt broke it by winning a third and fourth term. The Twenty-Fifth Amendment (1967) clarified presidential succession and disability, establishing clear procedures for what happens if a President dies, resigns, or becomes unable to discharge the office's duties. Each amendment answers a specific historical problem Article II's original text had left unresolved; you are not responsible for their details yet, only for recognizing that the executive branch's constitutional structure did not freeze in 1788.
