📖 Context Before You Read

This is the ratified text of Article II, Sections 1 through 3, condensed to the clauses Lesson 10.1 discussed directly. Read it the way you read Article I in Week 9: slowly, clause by clause, asking what design question each provision answers. This is a lighter reading-guide format this quiz week rather than a formal Critical Questions assessment; annotations are provided, and the questions below are ungraded discussion prompts.

Section 1 (qualifications, selected). "No person except a natural born Citizen, or a Citizen of the United States, at the time of the Adoption of this Constitution, shall be eligible to the Office of President; neither shall any Person be eligible to that Office who shall not have attained to the Age of thirty five Years, and been fourteen Years a Resident within the United States."

Annotation: The three qualifications named in Lesson 10.1: natural-born citizenship (with a one-time exception for citizens alive at the founding), a minimum age of thirty-five, and fourteen years of residency.

Section 2 (powers, selected). "The President shall be Commander in Chief of the Army and Navy of the United States, and of the Militia of the several States, when called into the actual Service of the United States… he shall have Power, by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate, to make Treaties, provided two thirds of the Senators present concur; and he shall nominate, and by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate, shall appoint Ambassadors, other public Ministers and Consuls, Judges of the supreme Court, and all other Officers of the United States, whose Appointments are not herein otherwise provided for…"

Annotation: Commander-in-chief power, the treaty power (two-thirds Senate consent), and the appointment power (Senate advice and consent), each granted to the President, and each checked by the Senate.

Section 3 (duties, selected). "He shall from time to time give to the Congress Information of the State of the Union, and recommend to their Consideration such Measures as he shall judge necessary and expedient… he shall receive Ambassadors and other public Ministers… and shall take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed…"

Annotation: The State of the Union duty, the power to recommend legislation, the duty to receive foreign ambassadors, and (last in the sentence, first in constitutional importance for this week) the Take Care Clause.

💡 Check Your Understanding

Answer the following for yourself or in class discussion. These are ungraded; there is no single correct answer to most of them, and no rubric attached. Their purpose is to make sure Article II's text has actually landed before you move to Wednesday's lesson on the veto.

1
The Take Care Clause comes last in a long sentence listing several presidential duties. Does its position at the end suggest it is the least important of the duties listed, or does something else explain why it comes last? Defend your reading.
2
Every power granted to the President in Section 2 above is paired with a check; Senate consent, a two-thirds threshold, or a limit tied to actual service. What pattern do you notice, and how does it compare to what you learned about Article I's checks on Congress?
3
Section 3 gives the President the power to "recommend" legislation to Congress but not to write it directly. Based on this text alone, what do you think stops a President's "recommendation" from functioning, in practice, almost like a proposed law?
