📖 Context Before You Read

Rousseau published On the Social Contract in 1762, twenty-two years after Montesquieu's Spirit of the Laws and roughly seventy years after Locke's Second Treatise. He wrote it in Geneva and Paris, deeply influenced by his memory of his native Geneva's small republican assemblies; a detail that matters, because Rousseau's theory works most naturally in a small, tightly bound political community, not an enormous nation of the kind Montesquieu accommodates and Madison will later defend. The first two passages below open the book and state the problem Rousseau sets out to solve; the third explains, in his own careful distinction, what he means by the general will as opposed to a simple headcount of private preferences.

"Man is born free; and everywhere he is in chains. One thinks himself the master of others, and still remains a greater slave than they. How did this change come about? I do not know. What can make it legitimate? That question I think I can answer."

"The problem is to find a form of association which will defend and protect with the whole common force the person and goods of each associate, and in which each, while uniting himself with all, may still obey himself alone, and remain as free as before. This is the fundamental problem of which the Social Contract provides the solution."

"There is often a great deal of difference between the will of all and the general will; the latter considers only the common interest, while the former takes private interest into account, and is no more than a sum of particular wills... when factions arise, and partial associations are formed at the expense of the great association, the will of each of these associations becomes general in relation to its members, while it remains particular in relation to the State... in this case there is no longer a general will, and the opinion which prevails is purely particular."

Jean-Jacques Rousseau, On the Social Contract, trans. G.D.H. Cole (1913 ed.), Book I, chs. I & VI, and Book II, ch. III. Public domain.
💡 Why No Full Excerpt This Week?

Unlike Montesquieu's chapter, Rousseau's argument for the general will unfolds gradually across many short chapters, and its key terms depend heavily on definitions built up earlier in the book. Rather than hand you an excerpt stripped of that scaffolding, this page gives you a careful, accurate account of his argument in context, anchored to his own words at the two points where a direct quotation is most illuminating and least likely to mislead. This is exactly the kind of source-handling judgment historians make when a text resists being excerpted cleanly.
