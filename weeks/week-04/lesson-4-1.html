Montesquieu and the Separation of Powers
📖 What You Are Reading Today

The Spirit of the Laws

Charles-Louis de Secondat, Baron de Montesquieu, 1748

Reading: Locke told you governments can lose the right to rule. Montesquieu asks a different question: how should power be arranged from the start so that no ruler, however well-intentioned, ever gets the chance to become a tyrant? His answer is the direct ancestor of the document you will spend Weeks 9 through 12 studying line by line; the Constitution of the United States.

🎯 By the End of This Lesson You Will Be Able To…
1
Explain Montesquieu's central claim that liberty is secured by the structure of government, not by the character or intentions of its rulers.
2
Define the separation of powers and state, in Montesquieu's own terms, why uniting the legislative and executive powers destroys liberty, and why an unseparated judiciary does the same.
3
Identify why Montesquieu treats the English constitution as his working model, and connect his tripartite division directly to the three-Article structure of the U.S. Constitution.

Suppose you could design a government and staff every office in it with a genuinely good person; wise, patient, uninterested in personal gain, devoted only to the public good. Would that government be safe from tyranny? Montesquieu's entire argument in Book XI of The Spirit of the Laws is built on his flat refusal to answer yes. "Constant experience shews us," he writes, "that every man invested with power is apt to abuse it, and to carry his authority as far as it will go." Even virtue, he adds, "has need of limits." This is not cynicism about any particular ruler. It is a claim about power itself: power tends to expand unless something built into the structure of government stops it. You cannot staff your way out of that problem. You have to build your way out of it.

This is the hinge between last week and this week. Locke asked what happens after a government breaks its trust; when the people may withdraw their obedience and start over. Montesquieu is asking a prior question: how do you build a government so that the moment requiring Locke's remedy never arrives, or arrives as rarely as human affairs allow? His answer is structural, not moral. "To prevent this abuse," he writes, "it is necessary, from the very nature of things, power should be a check to power." Not virtue checking power. Not law alone checking power, since laws are only words until someone enforces them. Power checking power; one part of government physically unable to complete a tyrannical act because another part of government holds the tool it would need.

Three Powers, and What Happens When Two of Them Marry

Montesquieu opens Chapter VI of Book XI (his famous chapter "Of the Constitution of England") by naming three distinct powers present in every government. First, the legislative power: the authority to "enact temporary or perpetual laws, and amend or abrogate those that have been already enacted." Second, the executive power: the authority to "make peace or war, send or receive embassies, establish the public security, and provide against invasions," and to carry out and enforce the laws once made. Third, what he calls the judiciary power: the authority to "punish criminals, or determine the disputes that arise between individuals"; that is, to apply the law to specific persons and specific cases.

Having named the three, Montesquieu makes the argument the entire chapter exists to make, and it is worth reading in something close to his own words, because no summary improves on it:

"When the legislative and executive powers are united in the same person, or in the same body of magistrates, there can be no liberty; because apprehensions may arise, lest the same monarch or senate should enact tyrannical laws, to execute them in a tyrannical manner."

Sit with the mechanism here, because it is easy to nod past it. If the same body writes the laws and also enforces them, that body faces no external check on either activity. It can write a law granting itself sweeping power, and then execute that law however harshly it likes, and there is no other authority positioned to say no to either half of that sequence. The lawmaker becomes, in effect, an arbitrary enforcer; free to legislate for its own convenience and then apply what it has legislated without restraint, because the very body that might object to the enforcement is the body doing the enforcing.

Montesquieu then turns to the third power, and his warning here is, if anything, sharper:

"Again, there is no liberty if the judiciary power be not separated from the legislative and executive. Were it joined with the legislative, the life and liberty of the subject would be exposed to arbitrary controul; for the judge would be then the legislator. Were it joined to the executive power, the judge might behave with violence and oppression."

Notice exactly what goes wrong in each case. If the lawmaking body also judges individual cases, it is not really applying a pre-existing law to a case; it is deciding, case by case, what the law will mean for this particular person, which is legislating after the fact, dressed up as judging. Montesquieu's phrase is exact: "the judge would be then the legislator." And if the enforcement arm also judges, there is nothing to stop naked force from posing as a verdict; the same hand that seizes you is the hand that convicts you. Either way, a citizen standing accused faces a power with no internal reason to be fair, because no other power is positioned to correct it. Montesquieu draws the conclusion for all three powers at once: "There would be an end of every thing, were the same man, or the same body, whether of the nobles or of the people, to exercise those three powers, that of enacting laws, that of executing the public resolutions, and of trying the causes of individuals."

He is not merely theorizing. He points to Turkey, where "these three powers are united in the sultan's person, the subjects groan under the most dreadful oppression," and to the aristocratic republics of Italy, where the same fusion of powers, he argues, produces a government forced to rely on secret informants and terror to maintain itself; a government no less arbitrary for lacking a single crowned tyrant at the top. Concentration of power, on his account, produces the same disease whether the concentration sits in one man or in one committee.

Why England, and What "Separation" Actually Requires

Montesquieu did not invent the idea that power should be divided; classical writers going back to Aristotle and Polybius had discussed mixed constitutions long before him. What Montesquieu did was look at a working eighteenth-century government (England's), and describe, admiringly and in close structural detail, how a state could actually distribute the three powers among different hands and keep it running. His England is somewhat idealized; he was reading English institutions the way a visitor reads a system he finds attractive, not writing a dry constitutional audit. But the idealization is instructive precisely because of what he emphasizes: a monarch holding the executive power, a two-chamber legislature (the body of nobles and the body representing the people) holding the legislative power in shared and separate deliberation, and juries of ordinary citizens exercising judicial power on a temporary, case-by-case basis rather than as a permanent standing body.

Crucially, Montesquieu's separation is not total isolation. Each power in his English model has a narrow, specific way of checking the others without swallowing them. The executive can reject legislation (a veto) without being able to write it. The nobles' chamber can block a law without being able to write it alone. The people's representatives control the power of the purse, so that an executive dependent on annual funding cannot become independent of the legislature that grants it. This is the feature later students of Montesquieu (James Madison chief among them) will call checks and balances: not three powers operating in three sealed boxes, but three powers each holding a specific, limited tool for restraining the others, so that ambition, as Madison will later put it, is made to counteract ambition.

💡 Flag This for Later

Hold onto this lesson. When you reach Week 9 and open the Constitution itself, you will find its first three Articles are titled, in order, "Legislative," "Executive," and "Judicial"; Montesquieu's tripartite division, restated as the literal architecture of the document. The framers had read Montesquieu directly; Madison cites him by name in Federalist No. 47, which you will read in Week 8, specifically to defend the Constitution's separation of powers against the charge that it violates the very principle it claims to follow.

One more piece of Montesquieu's argument deserves attention before you turn to the primary source itself: his account of why the separation of powers matters is not really about efficiency or good administration. It is about fear, and the absence of it. "The political liberty of the subject," he writes, "is a tranquillity of mind arising from the opinion each person has of his safety." Liberty, on this account, is not the absence of law, and it is not the ability to do anything you please; it is the settled confidence that no single person or body can turn the machinery of government against you on a whim, because the machinery itself is built so that no one hand can operate all three levers at once. That is a strikingly modern-sounding definition of freedom from a book written more than a decade before George Washington was born, and it is the definition the American founders inherited almost intact.
